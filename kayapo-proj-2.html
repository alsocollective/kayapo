Territorial Monitoring & Control

Government authorities are largely absent as far as enforcing protected areas including indigenous territories in the Amazon. The NGO partnership with the Kayapo provides the Kayapo organizations with transportation (boats, motors, trucks, fuel, equipment maintenance, overflight surveillance) and communications (short wave radios) they need to patrol their borders. In the works is GIS satellite imagery and technology for remote monitoring.

Satellite and over-flight monitoring that allows the Kayapo to see borders and pin point invasions.

Capacity-building training courses in territorial patrol techniques

Transportation and communications infrastructure, fuel and other supplies for border surveillance expeditions

Sustainable Small Enterprise Development

After 50 years of increasing contact, the Kayapo have come to depend on certain manufactured goods from outside society such as metal tools, guns, motors, fuel, glass beads and clothes. Loggers and goldminers take advantage of this need by pressuring Kayapo to allow illegal access to timber and gold on their lands in exchange for cash. The Kayapo decided they wanted no involvement with illegal predatory activities. NGO partnerships are helping the Kayapo establish their own sustainable enterprises. By developing their own small businesses that fit with their culture and natural environment, the Kayapo are maintaining their independence.

Economic autonomy based on sustainable management of non-timber forest resources such as brazil nut

Institution Building

The creation of Kayapo NGOs that function in national society has bridged the gap between the Kayapo and the outside. Their organizations give the Kayapo: a voice in Brazilian society (whereas without them, they would have no voice); the administration skills needed to access outside support; the means to bring together and unite leaders from far-flung communities so that they can devise a united strategy to meet the serious challenges faced by all Kayapo.

Building capacity of local Kayapo institutions that are necessary for accessing and managing outside support for conservation and development.

Providing a means for Kayapo leaders to meet reinforces traditional intercommunal bonds and fosters political organization.
