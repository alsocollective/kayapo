Why Rainforests Matter?

Once lost, it is gone forever.
Help stabilize the world’s climate by storing carbon, mitigating climate change, regulating rainfall and cycling pure water and air.
The forest produces half of its own rainfall and much of the rain south of the Amazon and east of the Andes.
Rainforests provide resources for people including food, medicine and recreation. Did you know that a huge percentage of the world’s diet originated in the rainforest?
Home to indigenous people and their unique cultures that live in harmony with the natural world.

The Kayapo have only been in contact with outside society since the 1960s. They continue to defend their traditional territories but are still learning how to negotiate the new realities of the outside capitalist monetary society that surrounds them. When the development frontier reaches indigenous people in full force as it has with the Kayapo, tribes who lack outside support to help them understand and deal with outside society are vulnerable to invasion by loggers, ranchers and miners and eventually, loss of their land.

Matter of Scale

It is larger than Iceland, more than twice the size of Nova Scotia, or about as big as the state of Virginia or Tennessee. Kayapo lands are as large or larger than half the countries in the world.

The Kayapo must defend more than 2,500 km of border of their legally demarcated lands against invasions by ranchers, colonists, loggers and goldminers.

Burning of rainforest produces about 15% of the greenhouse gases generated by human activity every year—an amount close to that produced every year by all of the world’s cars, trucks, busses and planes.
Experts estimate that we are losing 137 plant, animal and insect species every day due to deforestation.
That’s 50,000 species a year.
Only 1% of tropical trees and plants in the rainforest have been tested by scientists.
25% of Western pharmaceuticals are derived from rainforest ingredients.

Territorial Monitoring & Control

Government authorities are largely absent as far as enforcing protected areas including indigenous territories in the Amazon. The NGO Alliance with the Kayapo provides the Kayapo organizations with transportation (boats, motors, trucks, fuel, equipment maintenance, overflight surveillance) and communications (short wave radios) they need to patrol their borders. The alliance is also trying to provide GIS satellite imagery and technology for remote monitoring.

Satellite and over-flight monitoring that allows the Kayapo to see borders and pin point invasions.

Capacity-building training courses in territorial patrol techniques

Transportation and communications infrastructure, fuel and other supplies for border surveillance expeditions
